Cesspool Conversion in Hāmākua, Hawaiʻi Island

State census geography identifies Honokaʻa, Paʻauilo, and Kukuihaele as place names associated with the Hāmākua area. Wastewater planning should be based on property-specific information developed through the DOH IWS process.

If your Hāmākua property has a cesspool that you plan to upgrade or replace, DOH homeowner guidance says to begin by hiring a Hawaiʻi-licensed civil engineer to develop an Individual Wastewater System plan. The plan is informed by site-specific information rather than assumptions about conditions across the district.

What makes Hāmākua challenging

Site-specific wastewater planning. DOH describes Individual Wastewater Systems as on-site systems for treating and disposing of wastewater and lists approved treatment-equipment categories, including septic tanks and aerobic treatment units. The appropriate plan for a property depends on site-specific testing, a property survey, and the wastewater requirements determined for that property.

Property-specific evaluation. DOH homeowner guidance describes a process in which a Hawaiʻi-licensed civil engineer develops the IWS plan after site-specific work that includes a percolation test, property survey, and determination of potential wastewater requirements. Those property-specific findings, not district-wide assumptions, inform the proposed plan.

Before installation planning, use the property-specific evaluation and approved IWS plan to identify the work that applies to the property.

DOH wastewater rules and property-specific requirements. The DOH Individual Wastewater Systems page links to the agency’s Rules & Regulations and references HAR chapter 11-62 for percolation-test information. A Hawaiʻi-licensed civil engineer can use the applicable rules and property-specific findings when developing the IWS plan.

The process: same steps, weather-aware planning

Property evaluation. DOH homeowner guidance says the first step when upgrading or replacing a cesspool or failing septic system is to hire a Hawaiʻi-licensed civil engineer to develop an IWS plan. After a percolation test, property survey, and determination of potential wastewater requirements, the engineer submits the plan to the Wastewater Branch for review and approval. Review the typical cesspool conversion timeline while planning for Hāmākua access and weather conditions.

Plan development and review. A Hawaiʻi-licensed civil engineer develops the IWS plan using property-specific information, including the testing, survey, and potential wastewater requirements described by DOH. The engineer then submits the plan to the Wastewater Branch for review and approval.

Construction documentation. DOH says the engineer monitors construction and submits the final construction inspection report and contractor certification. When the required materials have no discrepancies, DOH issues an approval-to-use letter.

Technology and owner information. The DOH Individual Wastewater Systems page links to Allowed IWS Technology and to official homeowner resources. Use the approved plan and guidance applicable to the selected system for property-specific operation and maintenance information.
