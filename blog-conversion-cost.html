How Much Does Cesspool Conversion Cost in Hawaii County?

By Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help · Published July 8, 2026 · Updated July 16, 2026 · 7 min read

Cesspool conversion costs depend on property conditions, so a single figure will not fit every project. Engineering design fees, installation costs, and DOH permit fees together form the total project cost. (C-COST-A: NEEDS_REVIEW; approved wording removes specific dollar ranges)

Cost categories

A complete cesspool conversion involves three main expense categories: (Claim #24: MATCH across C-COST-A, C-DOH-A, C-CONTRACTOR-A)

1. Engineering design

Engineering design fees form part of the total project cost. The first step in the cesspool conversion process is to hire a Hawaiʻi-licensed civil engineer, who will visit your property, evaluate site conditions, and design an individual wastewater system (IWS) that meets state requirements. (C-LIC-A: VERIFIED)

The licensed civil engineer typically prepares the DOH wastewater design and permit application on your behalf. (C-COUNTY-B: VERIFIED)

Engineering cost factors: Conversion costs vary by district depending on local soil conditions, terrain, and system type requirements. Properties with difficult terrain require more complex designs and sometimes additional soil testing or surveying work. (C-COST-B: NEEDS_REVIEW; approved wording removes Kohala/Hilo-specific claims; C-DOH-C: VERIFIED for IWS types)

2. Permitting

According to the Hawaiʻi DOH Individual Wastewater Systems guidance, the Wastewater Branch reviews individual wastewater system designs and issues approvals. DOH permit application fees apply. (C-DOH-A: PARTIALLY_VERIFIED; role confirmed, specific fee range unverified)

In addition to the Department of Health permit for your wastewater system, you may also need county building or plumbing permits depending on your location and scope of work. County officials review excavation, plumbing connections, and electrical work (for ATU systems). (C-COUNTY-A: VERIFIED)

Permit cost factors: An individual wastewater system (IWS) is an on-site system for treating and disposing of domestic wastewater, most commonly a septic tank with leach field or an aerobic treatment unit (ATU). (C-DOH-C: VERIFIED) Advanced treatment systems generally have higher review fees than standard septic systems. (Claim #20: PARTIALLY_VERIFIED in C-DOH-A; ATU vs. standard cost difference acknowledged, specific multipliers not verified) Multiple dwelling units on one property increase fees as well. (Claim #21: PARTIALLY_VERIFIED in C-DOH-A)

3. Installation

Once you receive an approval to construct, hire a licensed contractor to install the new system and close the existing cesspool. The contractor schedules construction inspections using the IWS Construction Inspection Report form. (C-CONTRACTOR-A: VERIFIED)

Installation cost factors: Rocky soil can require specialized equipment, while steep slopes may need retaining walls or elevated tanks. Longer pipe runs add material and labor. Remote sites can also increase access costs for heavy equipment. (Claim #14: MATCH in C-COST-A under general property conditions; NEEDS_REVIEW) Properties with shallow groundwater, common in parts of Puna, often require aerobic treatment units instead of standard septic systems. Why total costs vary

A site assessment by a licensed civil engineer is needed to determine a property's specific conversion cost. (C-COST-A: approved wording; removes $20,000–$70,000 range)

Simpler sites with good soil and flat terrain tend toward the lower end of cost variation. Rocky ground, steep slopes, tight spaces, or advanced treatment requirements push costs higher. (Claims #26, #27: MATCH in C-COST-A under property conditions; NEEDS_REVIEW, no external source for terrain-cost correlation specifics)

Costs can differ between neighboring properties when site conditions differ. A site assessment provides the information needed for budgeting. (Editorial elaboration on C-COST-A approved wording; consistent with "costs vary significantly based on property conditions")

The State of Hawaiʻi has offered cesspool-conversion grants and tax credits in the past. As of this writing, the official Hawaiʻi DOH Financial Support Programs for Cesspool Conversion page lists no active programs and provides current program information. (C-FINANCE-A: VERIFIED)

Past financial assistance has included the 2023 Hawaiʻi Cesspool Pilot Grant Program, which provided up to $20,000 in reimbursement for qualifying applicants, and the 2016–2020 qualified cesspool income tax credit of up to $10,000. (C-FINANCE-B: VERIFIED)

The Clean Water State Revolving Fund (CWSRF), administered by the DOH Wastewater Branch, provides low-interest loans for point source and non-point source water pollution control projects. (C-FINANCE-C: VERIFIED)

Among common individual wastewater system types, standard septic systems generally cost less to install and maintain than aerobic treatment units. (C-DOH-A: PARTIALLY_VERIFIED for ATU fee difference; C-DOH-C: VERIFIED for IWS types)

A project schedule should allow time to obtain approval to use. (C-APPROVE-C: NEEDS_REVIEW; emergency conversion framing from Claim #6: MATCH in C-COST-A + C-APPROVE-C but partial match only)

Issues associated with delay

Delay can affect the condition of an older cesspool, property documentation, and compliance timing.

According to the Hawaiʻi Department of Health, cesspools are classified as substandard systems that dispose of wastewater without treating it. (C-DOH-B: VERIFIED) Older cesspools degrade over time and can fail, creating emergency repair situations. (Claim #29: PARTIAL match in C-DOH-B; degradation consistent with "substandard" classification; "emergency repair" framing is editorial extrapolation from verified fact)

An approved and permitted wastewater system is documented on your property records. Without a current approval to use, refinancing or selling the property may be more difficult. (C-APPROVE-C: NEEDS_REVIEW; retained as in original article; flagged for Ethan confirmation)

Act 125 establishes cesspool replacement as a legal requirement under HRS 342D. The DOH may issue enforcement notices under HRS 342D-9(a)(1) for non-compliant properties, and HAR Chapter 11-62-06(m) can trigger accelerated compliance timelines based on environmental risk factors. (C-ENFORCE-A: VERIFIED)

Getting a property-specific cost assessment

During a site assessment, a licensed civil engineer reviews the property's soil, slope, available space, and water sources. The engineer can then identify a suitable system type, define the design scope, and provide a realistic cost range before major work begins. (C-COST-A: approved wording; removes dollar ranges; C-LIC-A: VERIFIED for engineer role)
