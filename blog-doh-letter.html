What to Do When You Get a DOH Cesspool Notification Letter

By Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help · Published July 8, 2026 · Updated July 16, 2026 · 7 min read

A DOH cesspool notification letter means your property has been identified in Hawaiʻi's statewide cesspool conversion process. Save the letter, confirm the property information it lists, and gather basic records such as your TMK number, address, water source, number of bedrooms, and any old wastewater system drawings if you have them.

What does the DOH cesspool notification mean?

The Hawaiʻi Department of Health (DOH) sends these letters when your property has been flagged as having an active cesspool — an older wastewater system that absorbs household wastewater directly into the soil. Cesspools are classified as substandard systems because they do not treat wastewater — they only dispose of it.

The Hawaiʻi DOH Cesspools page reports that approximately 43,000 cesspools statewide pose a risk to water resources, releasing an estimated 53 million gallons of untreated sewage into the ground each day.

As summarized by the Hawaiʻi DOH Cesspools page, Act 125 (SB 542), passed during the 2017 legislative session and codified in HRS 342D, requires all cesspools in the state of Hawaiʻi to be replaced by January 1, 2050. Act 125 establishes cesspool replacement as a statewide legal requirement, enforced through HRS 342D and HAR Chapter 11-62.

The Hawaiʻi Cesspool Prioritization Tool is an online map created by the University of Hawaiʻi Sea Grant program and the state that ranks each cesspool by its risk to groundwater, drinking water wells, streams, and nearshore waters. The prioritization report defines four categories: Priority 1 (significant risk of human impacts, drinking water impacts, or draining to sensitive waters), Priority 2 (potential to impact drinking water), Priority 3 (potential to impact sensitive waters), and Priority 4 (placeholder for cesspools not yet fully analyzed).

The current map uses a separate three-level red, orange, and yellow display. See what a cesspool priority rating means for the distinction between the current map and the historical four-category report.

Do I need to convert my cesspool right away?

You generally have time to plan. Action becomes urgent in these situations:

You're selling or refinancing your property. An approved and permitted wastewater system is documented on your property records. Without a current approval to use, refinancing or selling the property may be more difficult. A cesspool can complicate a sale, refinance, inspection, or buyer negotiation because people may ask about conversion cost, timing, risk, and compliance. An engineer assessment gives the owner clearer information before listing, negotiating, remodeling, or planning future work.

Your cesspool is failing. Overflow, sewage odors around the yard, or unusually lush grass patches above the pit are all signs of a problem.

The DOH letter specifies a deadline. Under HAR Chapter 11-62, certain cesspools may need to be upgraded before the January 1, 2050 deadline. The DOH Wastewater Branch evaluates each property under HAR 11-62-06(m) and related rules based on environmental risk factors.

If none of these apply to you, you still have time. Starting early means more options and less stress later. Waiting until the final years before 2050 may result in fewer available engineers and contractors, as well as increased project complexity.

What are my options?

Option 1: Convert to a septic or aerobic treatment system

This is the most common solution. The first step in the cesspool conversion process is to hire a Hawaiʻi-licensed civil engineer, who will visit your property, evaluate site conditions, and design an individual wastewater system (IWS) that meets state requirements. An individual wastewater system (IWS) is an on-site system for treating and disposing of domestic wastewater, most commonly a septic tank with leach field or an aerobic treatment unit (ATU). The licensed civil engineer typically prepares the DOH wastewater design and permit application.

The Hawaiʻi Department of Health Wastewater Branch reviews individual wastewater system designs and issues approvals. Once you receive an approval to construct, hire a licensed contractor to install the new system and close the existing cesspool. The contractor schedules construction inspections using the IWS Construction Inspection Report form. After construction and inspection, you will receive an approval to use your new individual wastewater system. The engineer confirms that the installed system matches the approved design before submitting final documentation to the DOH.

Option 2: Connect to a municipal sewer system

The Hawaiʻi Department of Health's wastewater jurisdiction does not extend to properties already served by municipal sewer. To determine whether your property has sewer access, check your water bill for a sewer charge or contact the relevant county department. Municipal sewer is available in parts of central Hilo, Waiākea, Kailua-Kona (downtown area), Waimea, and some newer developments near Keaʻau — though availability varies by exact address. Most areas on Hawaiʻi Island do not have municipal sewer access. Homeowners should verify sewer availability for their specific address with the county Public Works Department.

What should I do first?

Do not ignore the letter. Even if you're not ready to act now, keep it somewhere safe. It contains information about your property's wastewater status that will matter later.

Figure out where your cesspool is. Note whether there are any signs of failure around the yard and what your plans are for the property long-term.

Talk to someone who knows the process. The cesspool-to-septic conversion involves an engineer, a permit from DOH, and an installer. Coordinating all three yourself is possible but stressful. Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help operates as a coordination service that connects homeowners with licensed civil engineers and local contractors. The service is supported by participating engineers and installers, so there is no additional cost to homeowners for the coordination itself.

Ask about costs early. Cesspool conversion costs vary significantly based on property conditions. The only way to determine your specific cost is through a site assessment by a licensed civil engineer.

Common questions homeowners ask right after getting the letter

"Is this going to cost me a fortune?"

Cesspool conversion costs vary significantly based on property conditions. Conversion costs vary by district depending on local soil conditions, terrain, and system type requirements. The only way to determine your specific cost is through a site assessment by a licensed civil engineer. Getting an engineer assessment before you know what you're dealing with matters more than guessing from online numbers, which are not reliable for your specific situation.

"Can I just fill the cesspool in?"

No. Simply filling a cesspool without converting to an approved wastewater system violates state law and can create serious health hazards. Act 125 establishes cesspool replacement as a statewide legal requirement, enforced through HRS 342D and HAR Chapter 11-62. The DOH requires that every property have a functioning, permitted wastewater system before a cesspool is closed — after construction and inspection, you will receive an approval to use your new individual wastewater system.

"What happens if I do nothing?"

Waiting until the final years before 2050 may result in fewer available engineers and contractors, as well as increased project complexity. An aging cesspool can fail at any time, creating property damage, health risks, and emergency repairs that cost far more than planned conversion.

Act 125 establishes cesspool replacement as a legal requirement under HRS 342D. The DOH may issue enforcement notices under HRS 342D-9(a)(1) for non-compliant properties, and HAR Chapter 11-62-06(m) can trigger accelerated compliance timelines based on environmental risk factors.

"Will this affect my property value?"

An approved and permitted wastewater system is documented on your property records. Without a current approval to use, refinancing or selling the property may be more difficult. An engineer assessment gives the owner clearer information before listing, negotiating, remodeling, or planning future work.

The bottom line

Getting a DOH cesspool notification letter is not the end of the world. It's an opportunity to take care of a problem before it becomes urgent. The sooner you understand your options, the more control you have over timing, cost, and outcome.
