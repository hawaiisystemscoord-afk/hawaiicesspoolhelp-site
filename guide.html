How the cesspool conversion process works on Hawaiʻi Island

Hawaiʻi has roughly eighty three thousand cesspools that must be upgraded or connected to sewer by the year twenty fifty. That mandate applies to every cesspool property, and the process can feel technical and unfamiliar. This guide shows the simple path and how Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help keeps everything organized.

The Get Help Now button at the top of this page stays visible so you can begin as soon as you are ready.

Why this guide matters

Homeowners often delay important work when three things show up together. Cost, confusion, and fear of getting pulled into a long process with no help. On Hawaiʻi Island the rules, soil, and timelines change from property to property, which adds to the hesitation.

Act 125 created a deadline, not simplicity. This guide gives you a fast overview and shows where Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help steps in so you do not have to manage everything alone.

The full process

Almost every cesspool conversion follows the same basic steps.

  • Step one initial assessment
  • Step two engineering and design
  • Step three permitting
  • Step four installation
  • Step five final approval

Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help stays connected to each step so the project keeps moving. You decide what happens on your property, but you do not have to be the project manager.

Step one initial assessment

What happens

A licensed engineer visits your property and looks at slope, soil, space, and the location of buildings and water sources. This determines what kind of system is allowed and what will pass review.

Your role

  • Provide basic property information.
  • Show roughly where the old system sits if you know.

How Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help supports you

  • We collect your details through a simple intake.
  • We connect you with an engineer for your area and help schedule the visit.

Step two engineering and design

What happens

The engineer creates a design that fits your property and meets health rules. This covers tank location, disposal area, elevations, treatment type, and setbacks. Some sites also need soil or elevation checks.

Your role

  • Answer a few practical questions about how you use the property.
  • Share preferences when there is more than one acceptable layout.

How Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help supports you

  • We keep an eye on progress so your design does not sit in a stack.
  • We help the engineer get maps and parcel data so you are not chased for the same items.

Step three permitting

What happens

The engineer submits the design to the Department of Health for review. Staff check that the plan follows the rules and sometimes ask for small clarifications.

Your role

Usually nothing beyond signing an application or owner form.

How Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help supports you

  • We monitor permit status so you know where things stand.
  • We help the engineer respond quickly if the reviewer has questions.

Step four installation

What happens

The installer brings equipment to site, excavates, sets the tank and components, runs pipe and electrical lines, then backfills and restores the surface as agreed. Some yard disruption is normal for a short period.

Your role

  • Clear access areas and move vehicles or items in the way.
  • Ask how the yard will look at the end and where you can drive or not drive later.

How Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help supports you

  • We help you connect with trusted local installers when possible.
  • We coordinate timing with the engineer so inspections and checks happen smoothly.

Step five final approval and long term use

What happens

The engineer confirms the system matches the approved design and submits final paperwork to the Department of Health. When it is accepted, your new system is officially approved.

Your role

  • Keep approval documents with your property records.
  • Follow simple maintenance guidance for pumping and system care.

How Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help supports you

  • We confirm when final approval is issued.
  • We point out which documents matter for future sale or refinance.

Costs in plain language

Costs change with slope, access, soil, system type, and how complex the job is. The biggest drivers of frustration are usually surprises and delays, not just the base price. Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help focuses on clear steps and coordination so you waste less time and avoid repeated work.

Why it pays to start early

Delays usually come from missing information, busy engineers or installers, permit questions, and gaps between steps when no one owns the next move. Those problems grow as more owners rush near the deadline.

Starting earlier gives you more installer choice, smoother reviews, and less pressure from crowded schedules. Act 125 sets the deadline at twenty fifty, but beginning now lets you pick timing that fits your life instead of reacting later.
