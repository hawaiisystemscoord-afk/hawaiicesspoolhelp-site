Signs of a Failing or Unsafe Cesspool

By Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help · Published July 23, 2026 · 7 min read

Direct answer: What warning signs may indicate a cesspool or onsite wastewater system problem, and what should a homeowner do immediately?

Warning signs that need attention

  • Sewage or wastewater backing up into toilets, tubs, sinks, or floor drains.
  • Several fixtures draining slowly or making repeated gurgling sounds.
  • Standing water, muddy soil, or wastewater surfacing near the cesspool, tank, or disposal area.
  • Strong sewage odor around the system area or inside the home.
  • Unusually green, spongy, or rapidly growing vegetation over the wastewater area during otherwise dry conditions.
  • A high-water or equipment alarm on a system with pumps or controls.
  • A damaged, sunken, loose, or missing cover, or soil collapsing near the system.

One symptom may have another cause. Multiple symptoms, surfacing wastewater, or structural collapse demand faster action.

Immediate safety steps

  1. Keep children, visitors, pets, vehicles, and equipment away from the affected area.
  2. Reduce water use to limit additional wastewater entering the system.
  3. Avoid direct contact with sewage. Do not walk through, hose down, or spread contaminated water.
  4. Do not enter a cesspool or tank and do not lean over an open access. Confined spaces and wastewater gases can be fatal.
  5. Call a qualified wastewater service provider, licensed contractor, plumber, or civil engineer based on the problem.
  6. For sewage entering the home, electrical danger, a collapsed opening, or immediate exposure risk, treat the situation as urgent and contact the appropriate emergency or public-health authority.

Odor does not always equal failure

EPA notes that an odor can come from a roof vent or an obstructed vent rather than a failed disposal system. A professional should distinguish a plumbing-vent problem from a cesspool, tank, disposal-area, or building-sewer problem.

Do not ignore persistent odor, especially when it appears with slow drains, backups, wet soil, or visible wastewater.

Why pumping may not solve the cause

Pumping can temporarily lower the liquid level, but it does not repair a collapsed structure, clogged building sewer, failed disposal area, hydraulic overload, damaged baffle, broken pump, or undersized system. Ask the provider to document what was observed and what follow-up is recommended.

A cesspool remains a cesspool after pumping. Pumping is not conversion and does not create treatment.

Document the incident

  • Record the date, weather, water use, fixtures affected, alarms, and where water or odor appeared.
  • Take photographs from a safe distance.
  • Keep pumping, service, repair, inspection, and disposal receipts.
  • Tell the engineer about additions, extra dwellings, occupancy changes, or drainage changes.
  • Preserve agency notices and property records.

When replacement planning should begin

A failure or unsafe condition may require immediate repair, replacement, or other agency direction. Hawaiʻi DOH’s homeowner process begins with a Hawaiʻi-licensed civil engineer for a new IWS design. The engineer can evaluate the property, coordinate required site work, and submit plans for review.

Even without an emergency, a recurring pattern of backups, pumping, odors, wet areas, or structural concerns is a reason to stop postponing a professional evaluation.
