Cesspool Conversion Timeline: What to Expect from Start to Finish on Hawaiʻi Island

By Hawaiʻi Cesspool Help · Published July 8, 2026 · Updated July 16, 2026 · 8 min read

You've decided to convert your cesspool, and now you want to know how long this whole thing takes. The honest answer is that every property is different. The Hawaiʻi DOH General Information and FAQs publishes historical averages for the conversion process: approximately 10 days for agency review, approximately 25 days for engineers to respond to comments, and approximately 200 days for construction and installation. These are averages across all projects, not guarantees for any individual property. Understanding the actual sequence of steps helps you plan around work disruptions and manage expectations.

The IWS process from start to finish

The official Individual Wastewater System (IWS) process follows a defined sequence with each step depending on the one before it. The total timeline depends on property conditions, engineer availability, DOH review pace, and contractor scheduling.

Step-by-step timeline breakdown

Phase 1: Initial assessment

What happens: The first step in the cesspool conversion process is to hire a Hawaiʻi-licensed civil engineer, who will visit your property, evaluate site conditions, and design an individual wastewater system (IWS) that meets state requirements. This includes assessing soil type, slope, available space, distance to water sources such as wells or streams, proximity to the shoreline, and the location of buildings and driveways.

The engineer designs an IWS appropriate for your property conditions. An individual wastewater system (IWS) is an on-site system for treating and disposing of domestic wastewater, most commonly a septic tank with leach field or an aerobic treatment unit (ATU).

How long it takes: Most cesspool conversion projects on Hawaiʻi Island take several months from initial contact through final approval. The total timeline depends on property conditions, engineer availability, DOH review pace, and contractor scheduling. Scheduling the site visit depends on engineer availability. If initial site data is inconclusive or conditions are unclear, the engineer may need additional evaluation before proceeding.

Where delays happen: Engineers managing multiple projects may take time to reach your property. Phase 2: Engineering design

What happens: The engineer takes all the site data and prepares a complete design package for DOH review.

How long it takes: For straightforward properties with good conditions, the design phase proceeds more quickly. Complex sites like steep slopes near Kona or rocky ground around Hilo may take longer because the engineer has to work through more variables and sometimes revise the layout.

Where delays happen: Additional testing requirements. If initial soil data is inconclusive or the site has unusual conditions, the engineer may need supplemental work before finalizing the design.

Phase 3: DOH submission and review

What happens: The engineer submits your design to the Hawaiʻi Department of Health for formal review. The Department of Health reviews your individual wastewater system design, and if everything meets requirements, you receive an approval to construct.

How long it takes: The Hawaiʻi Department of Health publishes historical averages for the conversion process: approximately 10 days for agency review and approximately 25 days for engineers to respond to comments. These are averages across all projects, not guarantees for any individual property. Complex designs or sites with unusual conditions may require additional back-and-forth between the engineer and DOH before approval is granted.

Where delays happen: This phase often takes longer than other steps because it depends on agency capacity and how complete your submission is. The DOH review timeline is outside your direct control. Incomplete applications or those that trigger additional questions add time to the process.

Phase 4: Installation

What happens: Once you receive an approval to construct, hire a licensed contractor to install the new system and close the existing cesspool. The contractor schedules construction inspections using the IWS Construction Inspection Report form. This is the phase most people picture when they think about cesspool conversion — the part you actually see happening in your yard.

How long it takes: The DOH's ~200-day construction average includes contractor scheduling, permit processing, and wait times — not just active excavation and installation. The physical installation itself depends on system complexity. An individual wastewater system (IWS) is most commonly a septic tank with leach field or an aerobic treatment unit (ATU). Simple septic systems are generally faster to install; aerobic treatment units with advanced components take longer. Weather can also affect scheduling: heavy rain makes excavation difficult and unsafe, so installers may need to reschedule.

Where delays happen: Scheduling the installer. Once you have permit approval, finding an available crew takes time, especially during dry seasons when demand is highest. Properties in remote areas like Kaʻū that are hard for equipment to access also take longer to complete.

Phase 5: Final inspection and approval

What happens: The Hawaiʻi DOH Individual Wastewater Systems guidance explains that after construction and inspection, you will receive an approval to use your new individual wastewater system. The engineer confirms that the installed system matches the approved design before submitting final documentation to the DOH. Once accepted, your new system is officially permitted and the old cesspool is formally closed out of active service.

How long it takes: If everything went smoothly during installation, this phase wraps up as paperwork gets processed through the DOH.

What your yard looks like during installation

It's worth knowing what disruption to expect:

Once you receive an approval to construct, hire a licensed contractor to install the new system and close the existing cesspool. Heavy equipment will be on site for excavation, tank placement, pipe runs, and backfilling. The exact sequence varies by property and system type.

After installation, some yard areas may need landscaping restoration. Most properties return to normal daily use shortly after installation.

How to speed things up

The DOH review timeline is outside your direct control, but you can influence how smoothly your file moves through it:

Work with someone who coordinates the whole process. Engineers focus on design. Installers focus on construction. If no one is watching the gaps between steps — making sure the design gets submitted promptly, following up on permit status, scheduling the installer before approval arrives — months disappear in those gaps. Be responsive when your name comes up. The DOH or your engineer may need you to sign forms, confirm property details, or answer a question about your dwelling. If you're slow to respond, the clock stops on your file until you do.

Waiting until the final years before 2050 may result in fewer available engineers and contractors, as well as increased project complexity.

When the timeline gets longer

Some properties simply take more time:

Properties near protected water sources (streams, wells, shoreline) face stricter review requirements due to environmental risk factors under HAR Chapter 11-62.

Steep or rocky terrain may require creative engineering solutions that need extra DOH scrutiny. Non-standard designs facing additional review aligns with conditional parcel review flags in the ledger.

Properties with difficult terrain — for example, steep slopes or rocky ground — may require additional engineering that extends both the design and review phases.

The bottom line

Most cesspool conversion projects on Hawaiʻi Island take several months from initial contact through final approval. The total timeline depends on property conditions, engineer availability, DOH review pace, and contractor scheduling.

Some projects may proceed faster depending on property conditions. Projects with complex site conditions may take longer due to additional engineering, review iterations, and scheduling.

The single biggest factor in keeping your timeline on track is not the engineer's speed or the installer's schedule: it's whether someone is actively coordinating between all the parties so nothing falls through the cracks.
